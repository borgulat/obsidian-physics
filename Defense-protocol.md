Hi all,

as it is a tradition to share the questions asked by the examiners during the defense with the current PhD students, here is a (almost complete) record of what they asked me. Some general information beforehand: I handed in my thesis in October and could start looking for a date for the defense in mid of January and found a date in march, so expect the defense to take place several months after handing in the thesis (in particular when planning in time for the defense preparation; I took three to four weeks off for this).

My committee consisted of Prof. Harlander (my supervisor), Prof. Czakon (second supervisor), Prof. Stahl (head of the comittee) and Prof. Feld. I chose Stahl in particular because I knew he was an expert in EDMs and I expected him to ask questions on this topic the answers to which I could prepare (which he did).

In general, the comittee members were really nice even when I failed to answer some very basic questions. Except for Prof. Feld they also only asked questions which were closely related to my thesis (or were even answered in my thesis). My thesis was about the Gradient Flow applied to the chromomagnetic dipole operator and the Standard Model.

My defense was in German, so I translate and shorten the questions accordingly. Each of the committee members had 10 minutes time for questions.

First was Robert:
- Gradient Flow is closely related to renormalization. When is a theory renormalizable? -> I said that you construct a Lagrangian from a field content and symmetries and renormalizability depends on the mass dimension of the coupling. Operators of dimension >4 are still technically renormalizable, but require infinitely many counter-terms.
- How do you prove renormalizability of QED, for example? -> Here I struggled a little bit, but he directed me gently in the right direction until I remembered the answer: You calculate the lowest order Greens functions (0-point, 1-point, ... 4-point) and show that they are finite using symmetry and superficial degree of divergence. Then all diagrams contain subdivergences of this form etc.
- How does the Gradient Flow affect this? -> Only replaces DimReg for UV divergences, IR divergences remain unchanged.
- How is the flow equation related to the Langevin equation? -> In the Langevin equation there is an extra stochasic term representing field fluctuations. I added some details about the history of the Gradient Flow and some of the first papers from the 1980s.

Then it was Czakons turn:
- You did not show us the full flow equation in your talk. Could you write down the missing term? -> I wrote down the gauge-fixing term.
- What is the relevance of this? -> I struggled a little bit to write down the gauge transformation which eliminates this term, but he was not that interested in this. I proceeded to explain that the term suppressed transversal momenta of flowed gauge fields and must be kept in calculations even though it drops out in the end.
- Why is the flowed gluon the only field that requires renormalization? -> I was confused at first, but he corrected that he meant why it does not require renormalization. I tried to draw a diagram for an intuitive explanation but could not remember the line of arguments, so I just argued that counter-terms for the flowed gluon are prohibited by flowed BRST symmetry, but allowed for fermions and scalars and wrote down the counter-term Lagrangian.
- You wrote about CP violation and applications of the chromomagnetic dipole operator in your thesis. Could you explain this in more detail? -> I talked a bit about direct and indirect CP violation in Kaon oscillations and decay and wrote down how the CMDM emerges from a penguin diagram in a kaon process.

Feld hit a weak spot:
- How many generations of fermions are there? -> Three.
- How do you measure this? -> I had no clue. I knew that it can be measured and I drew a Z->qq diagram, but he then lead me to replace the quarks by neutrinos. I sketched the decay width plot for the Z boson and he asked me how this is related to the Z decay. I mentioned the optical theorem which related the decay width to the total decay amplitude, but this is about all I could say and we used up the full 10 minutes on this question. It made me so nervous that I forgot if the Z boson even coupled to neutrinos. In the end, I could not answer anything really.

Stahl was last:
- Can you explain how the nEDM is measured? -> This is what I expected, so I explained how neutrons are cooled at the TUCAN experiment at TRIUMF and how the measurement works.
- Are there any benefits from using neutrons instead of protons, for example? -> Protons are charged, so you cannot apply a strong electric field.
- Are there benefits for protons as well? -> I thought about this for a very long moment and could not find any. We then discussed some ideas, but in the end it was the fact that free neutrons are unstable.
- Notably, he did not ask me anything about nobel prices (as he used to do) or stuff not related to my thesis.

I hope that this helps you. I was very nervous about the defense (in particular as someone suffering from social anxiety), but it was much more relaxed and kind than I imagined (it seemed more like a formality before confirming the PhD instead of an interrogation). I whish you all the best for your PhD and the defense. You will succeed for sure!
